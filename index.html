Why you should own chickens

No fluff. Here's the actual case.

A few birds in your yard who basically take care of themselves.

⏱️

The time commitment is almost nothing

After your initial setup, you're looking at an hour a week at most. Refill feed. Refill water. Collect eggs. Toss some bedding. That's the whole job — and most of it can be automated. You'll spend more time choosing what to watch on YouTube tonight than you will taking care of chickens this week.

💰

Feed cost is barely worth mentioning

A 50lb bag of layer feed runs $15–20 and lasts a small flock weeks. The eggs alone offset the cost almost immediately.

🥚

These are not grocery store eggs

Not eggs sitting in a warehouse for six weeks. Actual fresh eggs with dark orange yolks from birds you watched wander your yard twenty minutes ago. Backyard eggs are more nutritious — more omega-3s, more vitamin A and E, less cholesterol, higher beta carotene. The stuff on grocery store shelves came from a bird that never saw sunlight. It's not even the same product.

📦

You can set up everything from your couch

The whole operation is on Amazon. Small coop, automatic door on a timer, gravity feeder, heated waterer for winter, layer feed — all of it. Add to cart, wait two days, assemble on a Saturday afternoon. The barrier to entry is basically nonexistent.

⚖️

It's probably already legal where you live

Most cities and suburbs allow backyard hens. The trend has moved heavily in favor of backyard flocks over the last decade. Hens are almost universally fine. Roosters are where ordinances draw the line — but you don't want roosters anyway. They don't lay eggs and your neighbors won't appreciate the 5am wake-up call.

Quick start

New to chickens? Start here.

Five steps from zero to your first flock.

1

Check your local ordinances

Before anything else, find out how many hens you're allowed and whether a permit is required in your city or county.

2

Choose your breed

Pick a breed that matches your climate, egg production goals, and temperament preference.

3

Set up housing before you bring hens home

Have your coop and run ready — with bedding, feeders, and waterers — before your birds arrive.

4

Source your hens or chicks

Decide between pullets (young hens) or day-old chicks. Each has different startup needs — or let us handle the sourcing for you.

5

Learn the daily routine

Morning feed, egg collection, evening lock-up. A good routine keeps hens healthy and laying consistent.
