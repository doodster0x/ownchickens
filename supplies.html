Infrastructure tiers

Pick the setup that matches your budget

There are three sane ways to build your first chicken setup: buy it fast, build it cheaper, or source materials locally. Tier 1 is the easy Amazon route for people who want fewer decisions and faster setup.

T1

Amazon ready-to-buy

Fastest path. Buy the core system online, compare reviews carefully, and expect to reinforce weak points.

T2

Budget quality build

Cheaper for handy people. Buy lumber, hardware cloth, roofing, fasteners, and build a better structure.

T3

Local sourcing

Lowest cash cost, highest time cost. Pallets, sheds, marketplace finds, Craigslist, and local salvage.

Tier 1

Buy the whole starter setup from Amazon

This list is for a small beginner flock, roughly three to six hens. It favors convenience over perfect value. Do not trust prefab coop capacity claims blindly; compare actual floor space, run space, ventilation, and cleanout access.

01 / Housing

Prefab coop or coop/run combo

Start with the largest practical unit you can fit. Look for exterior egg access, full-size cleanout doors, real ventilation, and room to add hardware cloth.

02 / Run

Walk-in run enclosure

A walk-in run makes chores easier. Prioritize a roof or cover, enough height to stand, and a frame you can reinforce.

03 / Predator proofing

1/2-inch galvanized hardware cloth

Use hardware cloth on vulnerable openings and around the lower run. Chicken wire is for keeping chickens in, not serious predators out.

Search Amazon
04 / Security

Locks, carabiners, and extra latches

Upgrade basic coop latches. Raccoons can work simple closures, so use locking hasps, carabiners, or two-step latches.

Search Amazon
05 / Automation

Automatic coop door

Not mandatory, but very useful if mornings and evenings are inconsistent. Pick one with manual override and good weather resistance.

Search Amazon
06 / Food

No-waste feeder

Wasted feed attracts rodents. Look for covered, hanging, treadle, or port-style feeders that keep feed dry and harder to scratch out.

Search Amazon
07 / Water

Easy-clean waterer

Clean water is non-negotiable. Horizontal nipple or cup systems can stay cleaner than open pans when installed at the right height.

Search Amazon
08 / Cold weather

Heated water base or heated waterer

Skip this in warm climates. In freezing winters, plan water before the first cold snap.

Search Amazon
09 / Feed storage

Rodent-resistant feed bin

Keep feed dry and sealed. Metal trash cans or locking feed bins beat open bags in a garage or shed.

Search Amazon
10 / Bedding

Coop bedding

Pine shavings are common and easy. Keep bedding dry, replace it before ammonia builds, and avoid dusty products.

Search Amazon
11 / Laying support

Nest box pads or liners

They make egg collection cleaner and reduce broken eggs. Make sure they fit the nest boxes in your coop.

12 / Nutrition extras

Grit and oyster shell

Layer feed does most of the work. Grit supports digestion when birds eat anything beyond feed; oyster shell gives free-choice calcium.

13 / Cleaning

Dedicated cleaning tools

Use separate coop tools: gloves, scraper, small rake, bucket, and a stiff brush. Boring tools keep chores from becoming a whole production.

Small first-aid shelf

Keep gloves, vet wrap, wound wash, electrolyte packets, and a small crate ready. For serious illness or injury, use a poultry vet or extension guidance.

What to compare before buying

Tier 1 is convenient, but prefab listings can make tiny setups look bigger than they are. Check the actual numbers before trusting the marketing photo.

Item Compare Avoid
Coop Actual dimensions, ventilation, cleanout doors, roost height, nest access. Tiny coops claiming huge bird capacity.
Run Standing height, roof cover, door width, frame strength, anchor options. Bare chicken wire as the only predator barrier.
Water Cleaning access, freezing plan, capacity, height adjustment. Open pans that fill with bedding and droppings.
Feed Dry storage, waste control, rodent resistance, easy refill path. Open bags or feeders that spill constantly.

Own Chickens is an educational starting point. Always verify current rules with your city or county before buying birds or building a coop.
